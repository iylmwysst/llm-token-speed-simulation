40 TPS

How fast is 40 tokens per second?

For many chat-style products, 40 tok/s feels comfortably fast. It is usually quick enough to keep attention, but still slow enough that users can read while the answer is appearing.

By Pipat Khambun · Updated April 22, 2026

Quick take

If your goal is a responsive assistant that feels alive without overwhelming the reader, 40 tokens per second is a strong middle-ground benchmark.

Why 40 tok/s often feels good

Below this range, streaming can start to feel hesitant on longer replies. Above this range, answers can become difficult to follow live unless the text is simple or the user is mostly waiting for completion rather than reading in place.

That makes 40 tok/s a useful design benchmark. It is rarely the absolute fastest possible number, but it is often near the point where responsiveness and readability are both still working in your favor.

For chat assistants

Usually feels responsive enough that users do not question whether the system is working.

For coding tools

Good for commentary and explanations, though code-heavy answers may still benefit from higher throughput.

For long-form output

Often a sweet spot for readable streaming without making the response feel sluggish.

What 40 tok/s does not tell you

The raw number alone misses first-token latency, pauses between chunks, and answer quality. If a product is slow to start, 40 tok/s can still feel disappointing. If the output is bursty, the average can hide a frustrating rhythm.

That is why you should treat 40 tok/s as a benchmark to test, not a final truth to adopt blindly.

Best way to evaluate it

Run the simulator at 20, 40, and 100 tok/s on the same text. Read while it streams. Then ask whether your product should optimize for anticipation, comprehension, or pure speed.
